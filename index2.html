Key Findings

  • Financial concerns dominate non-applicant decisions: 24% cited financial aid package/cost of attendance as the top reason for not applying, and 34% listed it as a contributing factor.
  • Information gap is highly actionable: 35% of non-applicants felt they didn't know enough about Clarkson—this represents a significant opportunity to improve content and outreach.
  • Location presents challenges: Location (34%) and distance from home (29%) are major deterrents for non-applicants, yet enrolled families appreciate the rural setting for focused learning.
  • Personal connections drive enrollment: 43% of enrolled families heard about Clarkson through word of mouth, compared to only 12% of non-applicants.
  • Academic programs seal the deal: 65% of enrolled families chose Clarkson primarily for academic program offerings, followed by financial aid (55%) and academic reputation (52%).
  • The personal touch matters: Parents consistently cite hand-written notes, name recognition, and responsive staff as key differentiators.

Non-Applicant Survey Results

Students who inquired about Clarkson but ultimately did not apply were asked about their awareness channels and reasons for not applying. The results reveal opportunities to address perception gaps and information barriers.

How Non-Applicants Discovered Clarkson

Digital outreach dominates awareness among non-applicants, with email and web advertising reaching one-third of respondents. Personal connections—which prove decisive for enrolled students—reached only 12% of this group.

Awareness Channels Among Non-Applicants (% of respondents)

Primary Reason for Not Applying

When asked to identify the single most important factor in their decision not to apply, nearly one in four cited financial concerns—before even receiving an aid package. This suggests a perception problem, not just a pricing problem.

Top Reason for Not Applying
Key Insight

The second-highest response—"felt I didn't know enough about Clarkson" (14%)—represents a highly actionable finding. Unlike location or distance, information gaps can be addressed through improved content and outreach.

All Contributing Factors

When allowed to select multiple factors, the pattern shifts. Information gaps emerge as the most commonly cited issue, followed closely by location and financial concerns.

Survey 2 of 2

First-Year Parent Survey Results

Parents of enrolled first-year students provide insight into what drives successful enrollment—and what concerns remain even after commitment.

How Enrolled Families Discovered Clarkson

The contrast with non-applicants is stark: enrolled families are far more likely to have learned about Clarkson through personal connections rather than digital advertising.

Awareness Channels Among Enrolled Families (% of respondents)
Conversion Pattern

Digital channels reach non-applicants effectively (33% via email, 33% via web ads), but personal connections convert. Word of mouth accounts for 43% of enrolled families compared to just 12% of non-applicants.

Why Families Chose Clarkson

Academic programs are the primary driver, but financial aid—the top barrier for non-applicants—ranks second among those who do enroll. This suggests Clarkson's aid packages are competitive; the challenge is communicating that earlier in the funnel.

Factors in Enrollment Decision (% selecting each)

Parent Concerns

Even after enrollment, cost remains the primary concern for parents—followed by distance and the transition to independence.

Primary Parent Concerns (% selecting each)

Print Materials Assessment

While digital channels dominate discovery, print materials remain relevant for a subset of families. Importantly, those who do engage with print rate Clarkson's materials favorably.

Importance of Print Materials
Quality vs. Competitors
Print Summary

21% consider print important or very important. Among those who received print materials, 40% rate Clarkson's as better or much better than competitors, with only 2.5% rating them as poorer quality.

Summary

The most impactful moments across both surveys were personalized interactions: hand-written notes, remembering names, one-on-one conversations, and responsive communication. This "personal touch" is consistently cited as Clarkson's differentiator—and represents a competitive advantage worth protecting and scaling.
